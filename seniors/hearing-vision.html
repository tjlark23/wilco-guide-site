Hearing & Vision Care for Seniors in Williamson County

Staying connected to the world around you starts with good hearing and clear vision, and Williamson County has a strong selection of audiologists, optometrists, and ophthalmologists who serve the 55+ community. Age-related hearing loss and vision changes are common but highly treatable, and the earlier you address them, the better your outcomes. In Round Rock, several audiology practices near the University Boulevard medical district offer comprehensive hearing evaluations and hearing aid fittings with follow-up adjustments included. Georgetown's medical community includes ophthalmologists experienced in cataract surgery, glaucoma management, and macular degeneration monitoring. Cedar Park residents benefit from vision clinics along US-183A and hearing specialists who make house calls to homebound seniors. Many hearing aid providers in the area now carry rechargeable, Bluetooth-enabled devices that connect to smartphones and televisions, making modern hearing aids far more practical than the models from even five years ago. For vision care, annual dilated eye exams are covered by Medicare for qualifying conditions and are essential for catching problems early. When choosing a provider, ask about trial periods for hearing aids, financing options, and whether the practice coordinates with your primary care physician. Good hearing and vision care is not a luxury; it is a key part of staying safe, social, and independent.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does Medicare cover hearing aids?▼
Original Medicare Parts A and B does not cover hearing aids or hearing aid fitting exams. However, many Medicare Advantage plans include some level of hearing aid benefit, ranging from a discount to a fixed dollar allowance toward the purchase of hearing aids. If you have a Medicare Advantage plan, check your plan's Summary of Benefits or call the plan directly to understand your coverage. Some Williamson County audiologists also offer financing plans and bundled pricing.
How often should seniors have their eyes checked?▼
The American Academy of Ophthalmology recommends that adults 65 and older have a comprehensive dilated eye exam every one to two years, even if they have no symptoms. Medicare covers an annual dilated eye exam for people at high risk for glaucoma, and those with diabetes are eligible for an annual diabetic eye exam. Early detection of conditions like macular degeneration, glaucoma, and cataracts can preserve your vision and prevent more serious problems.
What are the signs of age-related hearing loss?▼
Common signs include difficulty following conversations in noisy environments, frequently asking people to repeat themselves, turning up the television or phone volume, feeling like others are mumbling, and withdrawing from social situations because of communication difficulties. If you or your family notice these patterns, schedule a hearing evaluation with an audiologist. Hearing loss often develops gradually, and early intervention with hearing aids leads to better outcomes.
Are over-the-counter hearing aids a good option for seniors?▼
Over-the-counter hearing aids, available since 2022, can be a more affordable option for adults with mild to moderate hearing loss. They do not require a prescription or professional fitting and typically cost between $200 and $1,000 per pair. However, they are not suitable for severe hearing loss and do not include the personalized programming that a professionally fitted device provides. An audiologist can help you determine whether OTC or prescription hearing aids are the better choice for your level of hearing loss.
